2 The Four Pillars — 四診 Sì Zhěn

These four methods are not separate steps run in sequence — they are simultaneous lenses applied at once. By the time a patient sits down in front of you, your observation has already begun.

⚠ Potential Quiz Trap — Count the Pillars

Schrier specifically pointed this out: "Observation, auscultation & olfaction, inquiry, palpation — wait, Daniel, that's five." No — hearing and smelling are one pillar together (聞 Wén covers both). The answer is always four.
👁️

Observation 望 Wàng

Diagnosis by Seeing

Spirit (Shén), body, complexion color & lustre, facial features, eyes, tongue body/coat. Includes constitutional observation — long earlobes (Kidney essence), upturned nose (weak Lung constitution), inability to make decisions (Liver/Gallbladder). Everything visible before the patient speaks.

👂

Hearing & Smelling 聞 Wén

Diagnosis by Listening & Smelling

Voice strength (Full vs Empty), breathing sounds, cough. Smelling: you'll ask rather than smell directly ("What does your stool smell like? Very offensive? Or minimal odor?"). Loud/foul = Heat/Excess. Weak/absent = Cold/Deficiency.

🗣️

Inquiry 問 Wèn

Diagnosis by Asking — NOT "Interrogation"

Schrier explicitly rejects Maciocia's term "interrogation" — we're not the CIA. Be curious: "Tell me more about how this is showing up for you." Covers the 10 Traditional Questions + 3 new Western questions + the 16 Questions framework (Week 7). This is Week 7's main topic; for now just know it exists.

🤚

Palpation 切 Qiē

Diagnosis by Touching & Feeling

Pulse diagnosis (the primary focus). Also: abdominal palpation (Hara in Japanese tradition), channel palpation, skin palpation, point tenderness (back-shu, front-mu, lower-he). Tongue is not palpation — it's observation. "Don't touch the tongue. That's just weird."

🏃 Ballroom Dance Medicine Connection

In a dance medicine context, Observation is especially rich — you'll watch how a dancer carries their body, asymmetries in stance, where they guard or collapse, skin color under stage lighting. Many dancers also arrive with habitual "performance face" — learning to see through the mask to the real Shén underneath is a clinical skill you're already developing.
3 Systems of Pattern Differentiation

The Eight Principles are the foundation — but they're not the only framework. Schrier summarized the full landscape of pattern differentiation systems this week. Think of it as a map of maps:

① Eight Principles 八纲 Bā Gāng THE FOUNDATION — today's focus. Exterior/Interior, Cold/Heat, Deficiency/Excess, Yin/Yang. Applicable in every case regardless of which other system you then apply.
② Qi, Blood & Body Fluids 气血津液 Vital substance imbalances. What Week 1 also touched on. Works alongside Eight Principles in most clinical presentations.
③ Zang-Fu Organs 脏腑 Zàng Fǔ The workhorse of modern clinical practice. Most diagnoses in TCM eventually land here — Liver-Qi Stagnation, Kidney-Yang Deficiency, etc.
④ Channels & Collaterals 经络 Jīng Luò 12 primary + sinew + divergent + luo + 8 extraordinary. Important for acupuncture point selection and channel-based pathology.
⑤ Six Stages 六经 Liù Jīng (Shang Han Lun) Zhang Zhong Jing's system for febrile/cold-damage diseases. Taiyang → Yangming → Shaoyang → Taiyin → Shaoyin → Jueyin. This is Week 3 territory.
⑥ Four Levels 卫气营血 (Wen Bing) Ye Tian Shi's system for warm/epidemic febrile diseases. Wei → Qi → Ying → Xue (deepening invasion).
⑦ Three Burners 三焦 Sān Jiāo Upper (Heart-Lung), Middle (SP-ST-LV-GB), Lower (KD-BL-LI-SI). Used in Wen Bing and some Zang-Fu frameworks.
⑧ Five Elements 五行 Wǔ Xíng Wood-Fire-Earth-Metal-Water generating and controlling cycles. Used heavily in Worsley Five-Element tradition; embedded in all TCM organ-emotion correspondences.

💡 The Big Picture: "Many Ways to Peel a Potato"

Schrier's phrase captures it perfectly. TCM, Japanese meridian/Hara, Worsley Five-Element — all using the same fundamental principles, different diagnostic entry points. They all put needles in people. They all give herbs. The goal is the same. The framework chosen shapes where you look first.
4 Critical Reasoning in EAM Diagnosis

One of the highest-yield conceptual points from this lecture. Schrier introduced three modes of clinical reasoning and showed how they interplay when building a pattern identification:

🔽 Deductive Reasoning

Start from a general rule, apply to this specific patient's presentation.

Fever + aversion to cold + floating pulse → you assume exterior pattern. Then verify with all accompanying signs.
🔼 Inductive Reasoning

Start from observed patterns across many patients, derive a general conclusion.

Historically, floating pulse = exterior pattern in most cases → becomes a general clinical rule (but always check the full picture).
🔗 Analogical Reasoning

Because X causes Y, the presence of X predicts Y will develop if unchecked.

Long-term heat damages Blood → opens a vector for internal wind → patient with sustained heat may develop internal wind pattern.

🩺 The Floating Pulse Trap — Schrier's Most Important Clinical Pearl This Week

A floating pulse does NOT automatically equal exterior pattern. Context decides:

  • Floating + exterior: fever, aversion to cold, occipital headache, stiff neck, runny nose — pathogen fighting at the surface
  • Floating + Yin deficiency: night sweats, five-palm heat, malar flush, afternoon fever, dryness — the pulse floats because Yin can't anchor Yang

Lesson: every sign is a question mark until you see the full constellation. Put a "?" next to floating pulse. Then gather the supporting signs before committing to a pattern.

🩺 Cold Limbs ≠ Cold Pattern (Always)

Cold limbs with a hot torso is NOT a straightforward Cold pattern. It's more likely Qi stagnation blocking warm Qi from reaching the extremities. The body core is hot — the stuck Qi just can't circulate outward. Context, context, context.

6 Introduction to Tongue Diagnosis 舌診 Shé Zhěn

Tongue diagnosis is part of Observation (望 Wàng). You look — you do not touch. The tongue is one of the most objective signs in all of TCM diagnosis: it changes slowly (days to weeks), reflects the chronic underlying pattern, and cannot be faked.

💬 Schrier's Clinical Protocol — The Murder Scene Rule

When scheduling tongue & pulse assessments, tell patients:

  • Do NOT brush or scrape your tongue before the appointment — "You're cleaning up a murder scene. You clean it all up, then we don't know what's going on."
  • No perfume, no cologne, no essential oils — these interfere with smelling diagnosis (Pillar 2)
  • When you look at a patient's tongue: poker face. Never say "it looks dead" even if it's alarming. "Oh, your chi is good" or "This is great diagnostic information, thank you."
7 Introduction to Pulse Diagnosis 脈診 Mài Zhěn

Pulse diagnosis is the deepest, most subjective, and most rewarding skill in all of EAM. You will spend years developing it. Week 2 is only the beginning — feel without judgment, describe what you notice, draw pictures if that helps.

Ibn Sina (Avicenna, 980–1037 CE) — Islamic Golden Age Polymath

"Only the musically trained can truly know the pulse. The pulse is of a musical nature."

Schrier's interpretation: like learning an instrument, pulse diagnosis requires your fingers to develop sensitivity over time. You don't have to be a musician. You just have to practice pressing on strings in different places until your hands learn what they're feeling. Schrier can play the Jurassic Park theme on saxophone — and he says that counts.

🩺 How to Record What You Feel (Assignment Protocol)

Schrier was very clear: you don't need to name a pulse quality to document it. Options:

  • Words you know: "Feels wiry at left guan." "Feels slippery overall."
  • Descriptive metaphor: "Feels like a piece of wood floating on a river" (that's actually the Floating pulse quality).
  • Symbols: +++ (very strong/big) · + (slightly elevated) · 0 (normal) · - (slightly deficient) · --- (can barely find it)
  • Pictures: Draw what the sensation feels like. Seriously.
  • Honesty: "I don't feel anything at this position" is a completely valid and useful clinical note.

7b Seasonal Pulses, Organ Clock & Pulse Timing ★ Schrier flagged this as a quiz question

⚠ Direct Quote from Schrier: "Good Quiz Question — Wink Wink Nudge Nudge"

"Wiry pulse of the spring, flooding pulse of the summer, hair-like of the autumn, stone-like deep of the winter." He said this verbatim and signaled it explicitly as something you need to know.
SeasonPulse QualityDescriptionWhy It Makes Sense
Spring 春Wiry 弦 XiánTaut, like a guitar string or bowstringYang is rising — Wood element, Liver, life is bursting forth. Tense/taut like new growth pushing up.
Summer 夏Flooding / Overflowing 洪 HóngComes surging like a flood, big and strongFull Yang, Fire element — everything is expanded and open. Pulse surges out like summer heat. Generally bigger, flooded in nature.
Autumn 秋Hair-like / Floating 毛 MáoSuperficial, delicate, like floating hairYang beginning to contract inward. Metal element — gathering, inward. Pulse is light and surface-level.
Winter 冬Stone-like / Deep 石 ShíDeep, hard to find — "like a stone sunk to the bottom"Full Yin, Water element — everything contracts and hides. Yang retreats inward. Students often can't feel pulses as easily in winter.

💡 Clinical Caution — Context Matters Here Too

A wiry pulse in spring doesn't automatically mean Liver disease. A flooding pulse in summer doesn't automatically mean Full-Heat excess. These are expected seasonal variations in a healthy person. You still need the full constellation of signs to identify pathology. Schrier: "Don't just say 'it's summer; the pulse is flooding, so it's excess Heat.' Look for the other signs."
Organ Clock & Daily Timing

From the classics (Suwen): ideally take the pulse in the morning when the Yang is not yet "too extended" — calm, not active. In practice most patients come after work, so don't be rigid about this.

🕛 Noon Clock = Heart Time

Schrier mentioned in class at noon: "What time is it? 12 o'clock. What organ is associated with 12 o'clock? Heart. So the heart pulse should be relatively strong right now — but that doesn't mean the patient has Heart excess. It just means we're in Heart time." The organ clock governs which organ's Qi is most active at each 2-hour window. This is context, not pathology.
Nanjing — The Source of Pulse Positions

Nanjing Chapter 17 establishes that pulse diagnosis is taken at the radial artery (cun-guan-chi positions). This is where our pulse position system originates. The Maijing (Pulse Classic) built on this. The Bin Hu Mai Shu (Li Shi-Zhen) is another major pulse text. For this course: follow what Schrier teaches in class and you'll be solid.

7c Tongue Diagnosis — Practical Factors & Medication Effects

Knowing the tongue map is necessary but not sufficient. You also need to know what makes a tongue reading UNRELIABLE — and what medications produce specific tongue changes that look pathological but aren't.

Factors That Affect Tongue Accuracy
FactorEffectClinical Implication
LightingUse natural light — artificial light distorts color. Classroom lighting is NOT good for tongue assessment.Always assess tongue near a window or with natural-spectrum lighting. This affects color accuracy most.
Diet before visitCoffee, tea, food all throw off the tongue coating and color. Can make coat appear stained or temporarily thicker/thinner.Tell patients: nothing to eat or drink for 30 min before assessment. No coffee or strongly colored foods.
SeasonTongue shifts through the seasons — Summer may produce a slightly redder, more active tongue; Winter a paler, more contracted one.Factor in the season when evaluating borderline findings.
AgeChildren: tonque generally more red (active Yang, but not necessarily pathological Heat). Elderly: tongue tends to be drier, more contracted, paler as Blood naturally diminishes with age.Normal age-related changes ≠ pathology. Don't over-diagnose Yin deficiency in every elderly patient.
Posture/effortDon't have patient force or strain the tongue out. "Nah" — like a kid doing the aspect. Just a natural, relaxed protrusion.Forced protrusion causes the tongue to tremble, deviate, or look stiff — can create false findings.
Duration5–10 seconds maximum. Schrier says 15–20 seconds is already too long. Ask them to stick it out, look, put it back, then ask again if needed.Tip → middle → rear (or rear → middle → front). Look at body color, shape, coat in sequence. Take a photo if helpful.

💬 Schrier's Clinical Story — The Antibiotic Reveal

"If you see a peeled tongue coat and you don't see any Yin deficiency signs, ask: 'Are you taking any medications?' And they say 'antibiotics.' You say: 'Ah, how did you know?' — like, wow, secrets. You just look like you know what's going on. Builds trust immediately."

🩺 Deviated Tongue — Schrier's Personal Example

A deviated tongue (deviates to one side when protruded) indicates Interior Wind. Schrier has a slightly deviated tongue from when he was punched in high school (defending someone from homophobic remarks) and dislocated his jaw. He uses it to illustrate: if he had Internal Wind, what signs would accompany it? Stiffness, tremors, sudden onset neurological symptoms, possibly headache. The sign alone doesn't make the diagnosis.

💡 Constitutional Variation in Pulse

Body size affects pulse baseline. A "tiny petite lady you could put in your pocket" can have a really big, strong pulse that would look excessive on a larger person. Always judge the pulse relative to the person in front of you, not just the textbook description. Pulse assessment is inherently personalized.
