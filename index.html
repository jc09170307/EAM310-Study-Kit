⚡ Chapter 30 · Eight Principles
FCM · The fundamental framework for all pattern differentiation in Chinese medicine
💡
The Big Picture Eight Principles = 4 pairs of opposites. Every pattern you ever diagnose can be understood through these lenses. They don't describe disease — they describe the nature, location, and relative strength of pathology.
Fig 1 · The Eight Principles Framework (FCM Ch. 30)

1 · Exterior vs Interior

🌬️ Exterior Syndrome 表证

  • Location: Skin, muscles, channels
  • Onset: Sudden, short duration
  • Key sx: Aversion to cold/wind, fever, HA, stiff neck, runny nose
  • Pulse: Floating (浮脉)
  • Tongue: Thin white coating
  • Cause: Invasion of external pathogenic factor

🏠 Interior Syndrome 里证

  • Location: Zang-fu organs, bone marrow
  • Onset: Gradual, long duration
  • Key sx: No aversion to cold, no fever (unless interior heat)
  • Pulse: Deep (沉脉)
  • Tongue: Changes in body color and coating
  • Cause: Emotions, diet, overwork, or exterior factors penetrating inward
🩺
Clinical Key The hallmark of exterior is simultaneous aversion to cold AND fever. The moment aversion to cold disappears and patient feels only hot, the pathogen has entered the interior. This transition is clinically important — it signals a change in treatment strategy.

Full Heat vs Empty Heat — A Critical Distinction

Fig 3 · Full Heat vs Empty Heat — critical exam distinction
🎯
Exam Pearl The pulse is the differentiating factor between Full and Empty Heat. Full = rapid + forceful. Empty = rapid + fine/weak. The tongue coating is also key: thick yellow coat = Full Heat; no coat (peeled) = Yin Deficiency/Empty Heat.

3 · Deficiency vs Excess

🕳️ Deficiency 虚证

  • Mechanism: Insufficient zheng qi (antipathogenic qi)
  • Types: Qi def, Blood def, Yin def, Yang def, Essence def
  • Features: Emaciation, lassitude, feeble breathing, pale face, spontaneous sweating (qi/yang def) or night sweats (yin def)
  • Pain: Dull, relieved by pressure
  • Tongue: Little or no coating, pale or red (Yin def)
  • Pulse: Deficiency type (weak, thready, fine)
  • Tx principle: Tonify (补 bǔ)

⚡ Excess 实证

  • Mechanism: Hyperactive xie qi (pathogenic factor)
  • Types: External invasion, phlegm, blood stasis, food stagnation, qi stagnation
  • Features: Sturdiness, agitation, sonorous voice, coarse breathing, distension/fullness in chest/abdomen
  • Pain: Distending/stabbing, aggravated by pressure
  • Tongue: Thick, sticky coating
  • Pulse: Excess type (forceful, slippery, wiry)
  • Tx principle: Reduce/Expel (泻 xiè)

5 · Complex Patterns

Interior-Exterior Simultaneously

Occurs when: (1) external pathogen transmits inward while exterior syndrome persists, (2) existing interior condition complicated by new external invasion, (3) internal factors directly invoke exterior symptoms. Examples: Exterior Cold + Interior Heat; Exterior Deficiency + Interior Excess.

Cold-Heat Simultaneously

"Heat above, Cold below" — stuffiness and heat sensation in chest, abdominal pain relieved by warmth. Due to Yin/Yang disharmony of various zang-fu organs.

Deficiency-Excess Simultaneously

Most common in chronic illness. Pathogenic factor overwhelms zheng qi → both excess (pathogen) and deficiency (zheng qi) present. Treatment must assess which predominates.

💧 Chapter 31 · Qi, Blood & Body Fluid
FCM · Pathological changes of the fundamental substances

Qi Syndromes

💡
Remember Four main Qi pathologies: Deficiency, Sinking (a subtype), Stagnation, Perversion (rebellious Qi). Sinking is always a consequence of Qi Deficiency — never diagnose Sinking without Deficiency present.

Qi Deficiency 气虚

  • Dizziness, blurred vision
  • Dislike of speaking, lassitude
  • Spontaneous sweating
  • Worse on exertion
  • Pale tongue, deficiency pulse
  • Cause: long illness, age, poor diet, overwork

Sinking Qi 气陷

  • All Qi Deficiency signs PLUS:
  • Bearing-down sensation abdomen
  • Prolapse (anus, uterus, rectum)
  • Gastroptosis, renal ptosis
  • Also called "sinking of middle qi"
  • Tx: raise and tonify middle qi

Qi Stagnation 气滞

  • Distension more than pain
  • Wax and wane, no fixed location
  • Related to emotions
  • Alleviated by belching/flatus
  • Wiry pulse (if Liver qi stagnation)
  • Cause: emotions, diet, trauma

Rebellious Qi 气逆

  • Qi ascends instead of descends
  • Lung: cough, asthma, dyspnea
  • Stomach: belching, hiccup, nausea, vomiting
  • Liver: headache, dizziness, hemoptysis, coma

Blood Syndromes

Blood Deficiency 血虚

  • Pallor/sallow complexion, pale lips
  • Dizziness, blurred vision, palpitations
  • Insomnia, poor memory
  • Numbness hands and feet
  • Pale tongue, thready pulse
  • Cause: Spleen qi deficiency, blood loss

Blood Stasis 血瘀

  • Fixed stabbing pain
  • Mass tumors (firm, fixed position)
  • Hemorrhage (dark, purplish, clots)
  • Ecchymoses, petechiae
  • Purple tongue / purple spots
  • Thready hesitant pulse (涩脉)

Heat in Blood 血热

  • Mental restlessness, mania (severe)
  • Dry mouth, no desire to drink
  • Various hemorrhages (epistaxis, hemoptysis)
  • Profuse menstrual flow in women
  • Deep red tongue, rapid pulse
  • Cause: invasion of pathogenic heat OR Liver qi → fire

Body Fluid Syndromes

Insufficiency of Body Fluid 津液不足

  • Dry mouth and throat
  • Dry lips and decaying teeth
  • Dry withered skin (no elasticity)
  • Scanty urine, constipation
  • Dry tongue (little/no moisture)
  • Thready, rapid pulse
  • Cause: excessive sweating, loss of blood, vomiting, diarrhea, dryness/heat

Retention of Body Fluid 水液停聚

  • Manifests as phlegm, fluid, edema
  • Fullness chest/epigastrium/abdomen
  • Heaviness, stickiness in mouth
  • Absence of thirst, diarrhea
  • Sallow complexion, edema
  • Types: Tanyin (stomach), Xuanyin (hypochondrium), Yiyin (anasarca), Zhiyin (chest)

😤 FCM Ch.20 · Internal Causes of Disease
Maciocia · The Seven Emotions in depth

FCM Ch.20 covers the same seven emotions as CAM Ch.8 but in much greater clinical depth. Key additional points from Maciocia:

When Does an Emotion Become a Cause of Disease?

Emotions are NOT causes of disease in normal circumstances. They become pathological when: (1) long-lasting (months or years), OR (2) very intense even if brief. Shock is the exception — can cause disease almost instantly due to its intensity.

The Chinese character ci ji (刺激) — the "stimulation" produced by emotions — contains the radical for water and means "to swash, surge" like a wave. Emotions become disease when they surge and carry us away.

😠 Anger (Broad Definition)

  • Includes: resentment, repressed anger, irritability, frustration, rage, hatred, indignation, animosity, bitterness
  • Bottled up → Liver Qi stagnation
  • Expressed → Liver Yang rising
  • With Kidney Yin def → Liver Yang rising
  • With Heat tendency → Liver Fire blazing
  • At mealtimes → affects Stomach (Wiry right Middle pulse)
  • After meals → affects Intestines
  • 🩺 Pulse MUST be Wiry if anger is the cause. If not Wiry, it is not anger!

😊 Joy (Broad Definition)

  • Not ordinary happiness — means overstimulation, excessive excitement, craving for constant stimulation (highs from drugs, gambling, thrill-seeking count as "Joy" in this sense)
  • Slows and scatters Heart-Qi → poor concentration, disturbed sleep, over-talkativeness
  • Long-term → Heart-Qi or Heart-Blood deficiency; if constitution runs Hot → Heart-Fire or Heart Empty-Heat
  • Sudden extreme joy behaves like Shock — dilates and slows the Heart abruptly
  • Pulse: Slow, slightly Overflowing but Empty (left Front position)

😢 Sadness (Detailed)

  • Includes grief and regret (mind turned to the past)
  • Depletes Lung-Qi directly via the Heart
  • Long-term sadness → Lung Yin def (night sweating, floating-empty pulse, no tongue coat)
  • Can also deplete Liver-Yin → Ethereal Soul (Hun) disturbed → mental confusion, aimlessness
  • Dr. Shen: unexpressed grief (no tears) → affects Kidneys
  • Pulse: no "wave" (Front position, pulse doesn't flow smoothly toward thumb)

😟 Worry (Broad Definition)

  • Maciocia: the single most common emotional cause of disease in Western society — chronic low-grade mental strain, not acute distress
  • "Knots" Qi in the Lungs first (shallow breathing, tense shoulders, sighing) then the Spleen (poor appetite, epigastric fullness) as it becomes chronic
  • Overlaps heavily with Pensiveness — Maciocia treats them as a spectrum rather than sharply distinct
  • Long-standing worry can secondarily knot Heart-Qi → palpitations, chest tightness
  • Pulse: right Front tight/Wiry (Lung); right Middle tight but Weak (Spleen) once chronic

🤔 Pensiveness (Broad Definition)

  • Brooding, nostalgia, obsessive/repetitive thinking, excessive intellectual or mental work (over-studying counts)
  • Knots Spleen-Qi directly — the "thinking organ" in Chinese medicine is the Spleen, not the brain
  • Manifests as poor appetite, epigastric distension, loose stools, fatigue, pale complexion
  • Distinguishing pulse clue vs. Worry: Pensiveness shows "no wave" ONLY on the right Middle position; Worry/Sadness show it across Front AND Middle

😨 Fear (Broad Definition)

  • Two forms: chronic low-grade fear/anxiety/insecurity, and sudden acute fright
  • Makes Qi descend — depletes Kidney-Qi, weakens the Kidney's grasp on Qi and control of the lower orifices
  • Children: nocturnal enuresis. Adults: incontinence or diarrhea after a sudden fright
  • Chronic fear → Kidney-Yin deficiency → Empty-Heat rising to disturb the Heart (palpitations, night sweats, malar flush)
  • Liver-Blood deficiency and Gall-Bladder deficiency can also predispose to a fearful disposition

😱 Shock (Broad Definition)

  • Sudden, unexpected event — even sudden GOOD news can cause Shock (it is about suddenness, not valence)
  • Scatters Qi — causes a sudden depletion of Heart-Qi, the Heart is said to become "smaller" and lose its mooring
  • Simultaneously affects the Kidneys (the two organs share a deep Water-Fire relationship)
  • Strong constitution → Qi scatters downward (fainting, loss of bladder control). Weak/Hot constitution → Qi rises (Empty-Heat symptoms after the fact)
  • Pulse: Moving (动脉) — short, slippery, bean-shaped, rapid, with a vibrating quality
  • Exception to the "duration or intensity" rule — Shock alone can cause disease from a single instant

Effects of Emotions on the Body — The General Mechanism

Beyond the organ-specific effects above, Maciocia describes emotional stress acting through several universal mechanisms, roughly in order of how a sustained emotional problem tends to progress:

StageWhat HappensTypical Clinical Marker
1. Qi disturbanceEach emotion first disrupts the smooth movement of Qi in its characteristic direction (rises, knots, scatters, descends, dissolves, slows)Wiry, knotted, or otherwise irregular pulse quality specific to the emotion
2. Qi stagnationIf the disturbance persists, Qi movement becomes chronically obstructed, most often in the Liver (free-flowing organ) regardless of which emotion started the processDistension, sighing, hypochondriac discomfort, Wiry pulse
3. Blood stasisStagnant Qi fails to move Blood — especially relevant in women (Heart/Liver/Uterus)Fixed stabbing pain, purplish tongue, hesitant pulse
4. Heat / FireCompressed, stagnant Qi generates Heat the way compressed gas generates temperature; sustained Heat becomes FireRed tongue (esp. red tip or sides), yellow coat, rapid pulse, irritability
5. PhlegmDisrupted Qi movement impairs normal fluid transformation → fluids condense into Phlegm, which can further mist the MindSticky tongue coat, slippery pulse; in severe cases, mental confusion or Phlegm misting the Heart

Not every emotional case runs the full sequence — many patients present and resolve at Stage 1–2. But recognizing where in this progression a patient sits helps prioritize treatment: early-stage cases need Qi regulation, later-stage cases need Blood-moving, Heat-clearing, or Phlegm-resolving strategies layered on top.

🏛️
Body-Mind Reciprocal Relationship Crucial clinical principle: Emotional strain → damages Internal Organs. BUT ALSO: disharmony of Internal Organs → causes emotional imbalance. Example: Liver Yin def from dietary factors → Liver Yang rising → irritability. vs. Chronic anger → Liver Yang rising. Both produce same pattern but treatment emphasis differs. Always ask: which came first?
⚠️
The "Five Emotions Turn Into Fire" Principle All emotions → Qi stagnation over time → compressed Qi generates Heat (like pressure increases temperature of a gas) → Heat turns to Fire → Fire harasses Mind → anxiety, agitation. Signs: red or dark red tongue, red tip, dry tongue — reliable clinical indicator of emotional stress.
🌪️ FCM Ch.21 · External Causes of Disease
Maciocia · The Six Excesses — deeper clinical detail

When Does Climate Cause Disease?

Climate becomes pathological only when there is a relative imbalance between the body's Qi and the climatic factor — either because the climate is excessive/unseasonal, OR because the body's Qi is relatively weak. A healthy person CAN be attacked if the pathogen is stronger at that moment.

Dr. Shen: the Chinese concept of "Wind" actually refers to rapid changes in weather — the body lacks time to adapt.

The Six Excesses — Full Pathology Reference

Each of the Six Climates (六气) is a normal, necessary feature of the seasons. It only becomes one of the Six Excesses (六淫) — a pathogenic factor — when it is abnormal for the season, excessive in intensity, or invades a body whose Qi is too weak to resist it.

Exterior → Interior: How a Climatic Invasion Progresses

An exterior pathogen doesn't stay exterior forever. Recognizing which stage a patient is in changes the treatment strategy entirely — releasing the exterior is appropriate early, but pointless (and potentially harmful) once the pathogen has moved in.

Clinical pearl: the single most reliable marker of the Exterior-to-Interior transition is aversion to cold. The moment it disappears — even if fever remains or worsens — the pathogen has moved past the Exterior stage.

⚖️ FCM Ch.22 · Miscellaneous Causes of Disease
Maciocia · Constitution, Overwork, Diet, Sexual Activity, Trauma, Drugs

🧬 Constitution (体质)

  • Determined by parents' Kidney Essence at conception + mother's health during pregnancy
  • Maciocia: constitution accounts for ~50% of lifetime health
  • NOT entirely fixed — can be improved within limits via diet, rest, breathing exercises, Qi Gong
  • Strong constitution signs: broad forehead, long nose, full cheeks, strong lower jaw, long ear lobes, firm muscles
  • Weak constitution signs: narrow forehead, small nose, thin cheeks, small ears turning outward, flabby muscles
  • 🩺 Scattered/Minute/Leather pulse + tongue without 'spirit' at root = weak constitution

💼 Overwork (劳倦)

  • NOT physical work — working excessively long hours for YEARS
  • Daily Qi (Post-Heaven) can be quickly replenished; Yin takes much longer
  • Short-term overwork → Qi deficiency (pulse weak, recovers in 2–3 days rest)
  • Years of overwork → Yin deficiency (especially Kidney-Yin)
  • Mental overwork → Spleen weakness (concentration, memorization)
  • Analogy: Qi = current account; Yin = savings account. Don't dip into savings to cover daily expenses!

🍽️ Diet (饮食)

  • Insufficient: Qi + Blood deficiency, Spleen weakness
  • Overeating: Spleen/Stomach dysfunction → Dampness, Phlegm
  • Cold/raw foods: Weakens Spleen-Yang → diarrhea, chilliness, cold mucus
  • Sweet foods: Major cause of Phlegm in Western diet
  • Hot/spicy: Generates interior Heat and Fire
  • Greasy foods: Creates Dampness
  • Vegetarianism risk: Blood deficiency, especially in women

💊 Medicinal Drugs + Recreational

  • Beta-blockers: pulse slow, deep — CANNOT be used for diagnosis
  • Tranquilizers: pulse "sluggish/reluctant" — not describable in 29 qualities
  • Antibiotics: tongue peeled in patches (Stomach-Yin deficiency)
  • Oral steroids: tongue swollen and red
  • Cannabis: Kidney def, weak Will (Zhi), poor memory (Spleen-Yi)
  • Cocaine: Phlegm-Fire in Heart (long-term)
  • LSD: Heart-Fire
  • Ecstasy: weakens Kidneys, obstructs Heart orifices

💞 Sexual Activity (房事)

  • Excessive sexual activity depletes Kidney-Essence (Jing) — the substance that governs reproduction, growth, and aging
  • "Excessive" is relative to age, constitution, and season — not a fixed number; the classics give broad guidelines only, not rules
  • Signs of Kidney-Essence depletion: sore weak lower back/knees, dizziness, tinnitus, premature greying, poor memory, weak will (Zhi)
  • Men and women differ physiologically in Chinese medicine's model — excess loss of Jing is traditionally emphasized more for men, excess Blood loss (menstruation, childbirth) more for women
  • Insufficient/repressed sexual activity can also cause disease — Liver-Qi stagnation from unexpressed desire, especially relevant historically for widowed/celibate patients

🩹 Trauma (外伤)

  • Physical injury — falls, blows, sprains, surgical trauma, burns — causes local Qi and Blood stagnation at the site
  • Local stagnation → fixed, stabbing pain; bruising (extravasated Blood); swelling
  • Old, unresolved trauma is a classic hidden cause of chronic localized pain that doesn't fit a clean Zang-Fu pattern — always ask about injury history
  • Severe blood loss from trauma → Blood deficiency; severe/prolonged trauma can also deplete Qi and even Kidney-Essence
  • Surgical scars specifically can create long-term local Qi/Blood stagnation even after tissue has healed

🪱 Parasites (虫积)

  • Historically a major cause of disease in China, tied closely to diet and sanitation — less emphasized in modern Western clinical practice but still part of the classical framework
  • Classic presentation: periumbilical pain that comes and goes, poor appetite alternating with excessive hunger, grinding teeth in sleep, pale patches on the face, itchy nose/anus
  • Parasites are understood to directly consume/obstruct Spleen-Stomach Qi and can generate Damp-Heat or Stagnation as a secondary pattern
  • Relevant differential to keep in mind whenever a patient — especially a child — has unexplained appetite irregularity plus digestive complaints

👁️ Week 6 · Observation — Spirit & Complexion
FCM Ch.23 · CAM Ch.9 Section I

Spirit (Shen) — The First Thing You Assess

Before tongue, before pulse — Shen is read the moment the patient walks in. It is the single fastest prognostic read available: a patient who "has Shen" has a functioning foundation to build treatment on, even if severely ill; a patient who has "lost Shen" is in danger regardless of how mild the presenting complaint looks.

✨ Has Spirit (得神)

  • Complexion: lustrous, has a healthy glow ("contained" — bright but veiled, not raw/exposed)
  • Eyes: bright, alert, vital — the classic marker Maciocia calls the most reliable single Shen indicator
  • Mind: clear, responsive, coherent speech
  • Breathing: even, unlabored
  • Body: moist, firm skin — reflects intact Stomach-Qi

🌫️ Lacks Spirit (失神)

  • Complexion: dull, lusterless, or an exposed/raw color with no "veil"
  • Eyes: dull, clouded, vacant stare, or unable to focus
  • Mind: unclear, confused, or severely depressed affect
  • Breathing: labored, weak, or irregular
  • Prognostic weight: this is a graver sign than most lab-style findings — treat as a red flag regardless of chief complaint

False Spirit (假神) — The Trap

A brief, deceptive flare of apparent vitality in a severely declining patient — sudden clarity, appetite, or brightness in someone who has been fading for a long period. Classically described as the "flaring up of a candle before it goes out." Recognizing this prevents the clinical (and family) mistake of reading it as genuine recovery.

🔄
What Maciocia actually changed He didn't discard the classical 10 — he expanded them to 16 by splitting compound categories apart (e.g. "Chest and abdomen" becomes its own item, "Head and body" separates into more specific regions) and adding categories the classical poem left implicit (Energy levels, Emotional symptoms, Sexual symptoms, Women's and Children's symptoms as full standalone categories rather than an afterthought). He also reordered so Pain — rarely a Western patient's presenting complaint gets asked about — leads, and Chills/Fever moves to a confirming position near the end. The content is continuous with the classical tradition; the packaging is modernized for clinical practice.

Maciocia's 8-Step Full Intake Procedure

  1. Let the patient speak freely about the presenting problem first, uninterrupted
  2. Ask specific questions to determine the pattern and establish onset
  3. Ask further questions to exclude or confirm competing patterns
  4. Broadly work through the 16 Questions
  5. Ask about past diseases and operations
  6. Look at the tongue, feel the pulse
  7. Ask about family illnesses
  8. Inquire about emotional, family, and work life to identify the underlying cause
🎀
Maciocia's own caveat "What matters is the general picture formed by the symptoms... it is only for didactic purposes that we list symptoms in isolation." Never diagnose off one isolated answer — a single finding (e.g. "no thirst") is a data point, not a verdict. Always integrate the full clinical picture before committing to a pattern.

Insomnia (Question #9) — The Core Framework

Good sleep requires the Mind (Shen) and Ethereal Soul (Hun) to be properly anchored in Heart- and Liver-Blood at night. Insomnia patterns split cleanly into two mechanisms:

Empty Type — Blood not anchoring Mind

  • Difficulty FALLING asleep → Heart-Blood deficiency (also consider Spleen or Liver-Blood)
  • Difficulty falling asleep + dizziness + blurred vision → Liver-Blood deficiency specifically
  • Waking frequently, excessive dreams, night sweats, palpitations → Heart- and Kidney-Yin deficiency with Empty-Heat

Full Type — Pathogen agitating Mind out of Blood

  • Dream-disturbed sleep → Liver-Fire or Heart-Fire
  • Restless sleep with dreams → retention of food
  • Waking early, can't fall back asleep → Gall Bladder deficiency (a specific, easy-to-miss pattern)

General rule: difficulty falling asleep tracks toward Blood deficiency (Heart/Spleen/Liver); difficulty staying asleep tracks toward Yin deficiency — though Full-type causes (Heat, Fire, Phlegm-Fire, food retention) can also cause waking.

Method for Taking the Pulse

  1. Time — ideally early morning, before eating, when Qi/Blood are least disturbed by daily activity (not always practical clinically, but the standard)
  2. Level the arm — level with the Heart, palm up, slightly supported
  3. Place the fingers — index (Cun), middle (Guan), ring (Chi), Guan finger lined up with the styloid process of the radius
  4. Arrange the fingers — spacing adjusted to the patient's body size (closer together for a small frame, more spread for a large one)
  5. Regulate the fingers — take an initial overall impression before diving into individual positions
  6. Move the fingers — lifting, pressing, searching, pushing, rolling to assess each position at multiple depths

Six-Step Pattern Differentiation Walkthrough

  • 1. Exterior or Interior? — aversion to cold + fever together = Exterior
  • 2. Cold or Heat? — thirst, pulse rate, tongue color
  • 3. Deficiency or Excess? — pain response to pressure, coating thickness
  • 4. Check for False Phenomena — True Heat/False Cold? True Cold/False Heat?
  • 5. Qi / Blood / Body Fluid layer — which substance is disturbed?
  • 6. Confirm with Tongue + Pulse — do they agree? If not, why?

When Tongue and Pulse Disagree Pulse changes quickly (minutes to hours) — affected by emotion, exertion, recent meals. Tongue changes slowly (days to weeks) — more stable. When they disagree, the tongue usually reflects the more reliable CHRONIC pattern, while the pulse may be catching something more ACUTE layered on top.

Why This Companion Matters

FCM's 28 named pulse qualities (Floating, Wiry, Slippery, etc.) come from the classical Chinese medical tradition. Hammer & Bilton's handbook offers a parallel, contemporary lens: instead of fixed named categories, the pulse is assessed along four independent, measurable axes — Rate, Rhythm/Stability, Volume, and Depth — which many clinicians find easier to teach and apply hands-on. The two systems aren't in conflict; they're complementary descriptions of the same physical sign.

The One Concept You MUST Know: Pseudo-Arrhythmias

🎯
A pseudo-arrhythmia is an irregularity that mimics a true Knotted/Hasty/Intermittent pulse but stems from a benign, transient cause — the patient shifting position, talking, holding their breath, recent caffeine, or normal sinus arrhythmia. Before diagnosing a real arrhythmia, re-check the pulse with the patient still and breathing calmly across multiple cycles. This concept appears in Week 3 (introduction) and Week 9 (deep-dive), and is a favorite practical-exam trap.
